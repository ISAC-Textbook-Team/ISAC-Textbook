The previous section addressed the questions of \emph{what ISAC is} and \emph{how ISAC has gradually evolved} from the perspectives of concept and technological evolution. This section proceeds to a more fundamental question: \emph{What is the core value of ISAC, and why can this value not be replaced by a simple superposition of traditional standalone systems?} We examine this question from several dimensions, including spectrum efficiency, hardware cost, deployment efficiency, and closed-loop service capability, in order to reveal the system-level advantages of ISAC over conventional standalone communication systems and standalone sensing systems. More importantly, this section argues that these advantages are not isolated local gains. Rather, they collectively point to a fundamental transformation in network capability: wireless networks are no longer limited to the single role of ``transmitting information,'' but are increasingly expected to ``sense the environment, understand system states, and support decision-making'' as part of an integrated service platform \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated,Saad2020Vision6G}. In this sense, ISAC should not be viewed as an optional add-on, but as one of the intrinsic capabilities of future 6G networks.

\subsection{Why Is ISAC Not Merely an ``Optional Addition to Existing Functions''?}
\label{subsec:ch1-isac-core-value-why}
\subsubsection{The Value Logic from Standalone Systems to Integrated Systems}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core-value-logic}

Over the past decades, communication systems and sensing systems have developed along two distinct and largely independent technological trajectories, each forming its own mature theoretical framework and engineering ecosystem. The core task of a communication system has always been to enable reliable and efficient information transmission between different nodes. Its design priorities are therefore centered on channel capacity, spectral efficiency, transmission reliability, coverage, and latency \cite{Goldsmith2005}. Whether one considers the generational evolution from 1G to 5G, or the development of key technologies from FDMA and TDMA to OFDMA and massive MIMO, the fundamental objective of communication systems can be summarized as ``how to transmit bits more efficiently.'' In this sense, conventional communication systems interact with the external physical world only indirectly: they care about how the propagation environment affects link quality, rather than directly representing the semantics, structure, or dynamics of the environment itself.

In contrast, standalone sensing systems---especially radar, localization, and environmental monitoring systems---take the understanding of the physical world as their primary objective. Their design goals are centered on target detection, parameter estimation, state tracking, and scene perception \cite{SkolnikRadarBook}. Such systems are concerned with questions such as where the target is located, how fast it is moving, what scatterers or obstacles exist in the environment, and how the scene evolves over time. However, traditional sensing systems typically do not directly provide networked delivery of the acquired information to remote nodes, nor are they inherently integrated with upper-layer communication, computing, and control processes. In other words, throughout a long period of technological evolution, communication systems and sensing systems respectively assumed two related but engineering-wise separate roles: ``conveying information'' and ``observing the environment.''

This division of labor was reasonable and efficient for a long time. The main reason is that early network services could often be decomposed into relatively independent functional modules: communication systems provided connectivity and data delivery, sensing systems provided measurement and detection, and decision-making and control were usually handled by dedicated systems at higher layers. As long as the interfaces between systems were sufficiently clear and the task boundaries sufficiently explicit, such a functionally decoupled design could provide good modularity benefits while reducing system design complexity. However, with the changing nature of future wireless network applications, this logic of separation is encountering fundamental challenges \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Saad2020Vision6G}.

Many key scenarios envisioned for 6G and beyond are no longer satisfied with merely ``information transmission'' or merely ``environment detection.'' Instead, they require the system to possess information transmission capability, environmental awareness capability, and real-time feedback and collaborative decision-making capability \emph{simultaneously}, and to tightly couple these capabilities within a unified system framework \cite{Gonzalez2024integrated,IMT2030Framework}. Consider autonomous driving as an example. A vehicle must not only sense surrounding vehicles, pedestrians, obstacles, and lane boundaries in real time, but also share information with other vehicles, roadside infrastructure, and edge computing nodes through V2X links, in order to perform path planning, risk prediction, and control execution. In this process, sensing results directly affect the priority of communication content, link configuration, and resource scheduling; meanwhile, the information delivered through communication extends the spatial range of local perception, compensates for the limitations of a single-vehicle viewpoint, and improves the completeness of overall scene understanding \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Zhang2020perceptive}. Therefore, in such scenarios, sensing and communication are no longer two independent tasks that can be separately completed and then simply concatenated. They become mutually dependent and mutually reinforcing components within a unified service closed loop.

The same logic applies to scenarios such as the industrial Internet, low-altitude collaborative networks, intelligent transportation, robot cooperation, and digital twins \cite{Saad2020Vision6G,Khan2022digital}. In these scenarios, the system is not facing a single ``transmission task'' or ``detection task,'' but rather a comprehensive task chain involving physical-state sensing, information interaction, joint decision-making, and real-time control. If one still adopts a loosely coupled architecture of ``standalone communication system + standalone sensing system + external decision system,'' the system will inevitably suffer from complex interfaces, accumulated latency, redundant information, duplicated resource occupation, and difficulty in achieving global optimization. This is especially problematic in scenarios requiring millisecond-level or even lower-latency control loops, where environmental sensing and link scheduling must be tightly coordinated. In such settings, loosely coupled architectures can hardly meet future network requirements in terms of real-time responsiveness, consistency, and resource efficiency \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Cui2024integrated}.

It is precisely in this context that the core value of ISAC emerges. ISAC does not simply mean ``adding a sensing function to a communication system,'' nor does it merely mean ``allowing one hardware platform to perform two tasks incidentally.'' Rather, it means opening up the originally separated chains of sensing, communication, and service decision support, thereby upgrading wireless networks from pure information pipes into integrated service platforms capable of sensing the environment, understanding system states, and supporting task closed loops \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated}. This value is fundamentally system-level in nature: communication is no longer just a transport channel for sensing results, and sensing is no longer merely an external input source to the network. Instead, through unified design, the two form a mutually empowering capability system that jointly influences resource scheduling, link management, task orchestration, and service execution.

For this reason, ISAC cannot be simply understood as an ``optional addition'' to existing functions. If one merely places an independent sensing device alongside a communication system, or appends a communication link to a sensing system, then although the resulting arrangement may appear to realize both communication and sensing functions, such loose concatenation usually cannot support unified time-frequency-space resource management, unified protocol scheduling, or unified service orchestration. More importantly, it can hardly satisfy the future requirements of low latency, high consistency, and high resource utilization \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC}. Fundamentally, the irreplaceability of ISAC does not lie in the fact that it ``does one more thing,'' but in the fact that it changes the way system capabilities are organized: it integrates originally separate communication, sensing, and partial decision-support capabilities into a unified framework, thereby enabling system-level synergy gains that are difficult for conventional decoupled systems to achieve.

\subsubsection{The Core Value of Restructuring System-Level Benefits}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core-value-benefit}

A common misunderstanding when evaluating ISAC is to judge its value solely by a single performance metric, for example, by claiming that ISAC is valuable only if its sensing accuracy exceeds that of a standalone radar, or only if its communication rate exceeds that of a standalone communication system. Such a view is neither accurate nor generally valid. In fact, if one compares systems from the perspective of a single isolated function, then a standalone radar designed specifically for sensing may indeed outperform the sensing module of an ISAC system in terms of detection range, parameter estimation accuracy, target detection performance, or clutter suppression capability. Likewise, a standalone communication system optimized exclusively for data transmission may outperform the communication module of an ISAC system in throughput, bit error rate, coding and modulation flexibility, or link optimization capability \cite{Goldsmith2005,Liu2022ISAC,SkolnikRadarBook}. This is because a standalone system can devote all available spectrum, power, time, and processing resources to one objective, whereas an ISAC system must simultaneously support both communication and sensing under a unified resource budget. Its design is therefore inherently a problem of multi-objective tradeoff and collaborative optimization.

Accordingly, the true value of ISAC is not primarily reflected in the complete superiority of some individual function over traditional dedicated systems. Rather, it lies in the reorganization and reconstruction of the system-wide benefit structure. In other words, ISAC changes not a single local performance indicator, but the overall system utility function in terms of resource usage, functional organization, and service support \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated}. This can be understood as follows: if the optimization objective of conventional standalone systems is to separately pursue ``optimal communication system performance'' and ``optimal sensing system performance,'' then the optimization objective of ISAC is to pursue ``optimal integrated service effectiveness'' on a unified platform. From a systems engineering perspective, this shift implies that the evaluation criterion must also move from single-function optimality to system-level optimality.

More specifically, the restructuring of system-level benefits brought by ISAC is mainly reflected in the following aspects.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{Overall improvement in resource utilization.}  
Under the traditional paradigm, communication systems and sensing systems usually occupy separate spectrum, hardware platforms, time resources, and deployment space, resulting in fragmented resource usage and duplicated investment. Under the ISAC framework, however, the same spectrum segment, the same RF front-end, the same antenna array, and even the same time-frequency resource block can be jointly used for communication and sensing tasks, thereby significantly improving system-level resource utilization efficiency \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC,Hassanien2015dual}. This gain should not be simplistically interpreted as ``doing two things with one unit of resources.'' More accurately, it reflects the reduction of resource redundancy through joint design, the alleviation of spectrum scarcity, and the creation of new possibilities for multi-task coordination in future dense networks.

\item \textbf{Significant reduction in total system cost.}  
Another core value of ISAC lies in reducing the overall cost of system construction and operation through hardware sharing, site reuse, protocol-stack coordination, and infrastructure reuse. If communication and sensing are built as separate platforms, then RF chains, antennas, synchronization modules, computing platforms, and power supply systems must be repeatedly provisioned, and additional deployment, calibration, maintenance, and upgrade costs will also arise. In contrast, ISAC tends to introduce sensing capabilities in an evolutionary manner on top of existing cellular networks, vehicular infrastructure, or local wireless platforms, which makes it more economically feasible for large-scale deployment. It should be emphasized that ``cost reduction'' here does not mean that the design of a single device necessarily becomes simpler. In many cases, the transceiver and algorithm design of ISAC is actually more complex. However, from the perspective of total cost of ownership and long-term scalable deployment, integrated architectures often have more evident advantages \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Wen2024survey}.

\item \textbf{Enhanced deployment flexibility and scalability.}  
Since ISAC can provide sensing capability directly on top of existing communication network infrastructure, it offers greater flexibility for the rapid introduction and large-scale expansion of new services. For example, in cellular networks, Wi-Fi networks, vehicle--road collaborative networks, or low-altitude communication networks, if the nodes already possess strong signal transmission, reception, and multi-antenna processing capabilities, then environmental sensing functions may be introduced through protocol upgrades, waveform redesign, and algorithmic enhancement, without requiring a complete reconstruction of the hardware platform \cite{Ma2022WiFiSensingSurvey}. This means that ISAC is not only a ``performance optimization technology,'' but also a ``capability evolution mechanism'': it enables wireless networks to gradually evolve from pure connectivity platforms into integrated information infrastructures that support both connectivity and environmental cognition.

\item \textbf{Qualitative transformation of service capability.}  
At a deeper level, the most irreplaceable value of ISAC lies in the qualitative transformation it induces in network service capability. Traditional communication systems mainly provide information transfer, while traditional sensing systems mainly provide environmental measurement. ISAC, however, enables a unified closed-loop chain of ``sensing--communication--decision--feedback.'' This closed-loop capability means that the network is no longer merely forwarding data passively, nor merely producing isolated sensing outputs. Instead, it can dynamically integrate environmental information, link states, and service requirements around task objectives, thereby supporting higher-level control, coordination, and intelligent decision-making. For example, in scenarios such as autonomous driving, industrial automation, robot collaboration, UAV swarms, and digital twins, what applications truly require is not isolated ``high-accuracy sensing'' or isolated ``high-throughput communication,'' but a unified service loop capable of continuous sensing, timely sharing, rapid decision-making, and real-time feedback \cite{Gonzalez2024integrated,Saad2020Vision6G}. In this sense, the value brought by ISAC is not the local enhancement of one functional module, but a systematic upgrade of the service paradigm.
\end{enumerate}

It follows that the value of ISAC cannot be evaluated by simply reusing the single-metric evaluation frameworks of traditional communication systems or traditional radar systems, nor by using ``whether it outperforms a dedicated system in some single metric'' as the criterion. A more reasonable evaluation should comprehensively examine the integrated benefits it brings in terms of resource efficiency, total cost, deployment evolution capability, and support for service closed loops \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated}. In other words, the key significance of ISAC does not lie in pushing both communication and sensing to their respective extremes, but in redefining what constitutes a better system on a unified platform. The following four subsections discuss this irreplaceable value along the dimensions of spectrum efficiency, hardware and cost, deployment efficiency, and closed-loop service capability.

\subsection{Spectrum Efficiency Advantage}
\label{subsec:ch1-isac-core-value-spectrum}
\subsubsection{The Spectrum Occupation Problem of Traditional Standalone Systems}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core-value-spectrum-occupation}

Spectrum resources are the fundamental physical carrier of wireless systems and one of the scarcest strategic resources in the modern information society. Whether in communications, radar, navigation, satellite remote sensing, unmanned systems, or industrial wireless control, the realization of core functions depends critically on the occupation and management of limited electromagnetic spectrum resources \cite{Goldsmith2005}. However, for a long time, communication systems and sensing systems have largely followed separate spectrum allocation philosophies: different services are assigned to relatively independent frequency bands according to predefined rules, and mutual interference is avoided by static or semi-static frequency separation. This approach was engineering-wise reasonable when systems were small in scale, service types were limited, and cross-system coordination was weak, because it simplified regulation, device design, and interference management. Yet, as wireless service density and system heterogeneity continue to increase, the resulting inefficiency in spectrum usage has become increasingly evident \cite{Gonzalez2024integrated}.

From the global perspective of spectrum occupation, radar systems have long occupied important positions in multiple key frequency bands, especially in traditional microwave bands such as the L, S, C, and X bands, where aviation surveillance radars, weather radars, maritime radars, military early-warning radars, and automotive radars are widely deployed \cite{SkolnikRadarBook}. At the same time, the demand for spectrum from cellular mobile communications, wireless local area networks, satellite Internet, and various emerging wireless access services continues to grow. In particular, driven by the visions of 5G-Advanced and 6G, networks are expected to simultaneously support enhanced mobile broadband, ultra-low-latency connectivity, massive machine access, and intelligent environmental sensing, which further intensifies spectrum scarcity \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Saad2020Vision6G,IMT2030Framework}. On the demand side, communication systems seek wider contiguous bandwidth to support higher data rates and lower latency; on the supply side, however, high-quality spectrum that is suitable for wireless propagation, feasible in hardware, and permitted by regulation remains inherently limited. As a result, the traditional paradigm of exclusive and separated spectrum allocation is becoming increasingly incompatible with the requirements of future networks.

More importantly, in traditional standalone systems, ``occupying'' spectrum does not necessarily mean ``using it efficiently.'' Many radar systems, especially pulsed radars, exhibit strong intermittent transmission characteristics: in the time domain, silent intervals exist between pulses; in the spatial domain, beams often illuminate only specific directions; and in the task domain, operating states vary with scan cycles, detection modes, and target activity \cite{SkolnikRadarBook}. Therefore, even if a certain band is dedicated to radar service at the regulatory level, this does not imply that the band is fully utilized at all times, in all locations, and in all directions. Similarly, communication systems also exhibit significant spatiotemporal imbalance in spectrum utilization. For example, the traffic load of a cellular base station may differ by several times or even orders of magnitude between day and night, urban and suburban areas, or hotspot and non-hotspot regions. Some allocated bands may be lightly used in specific areas or time periods, yet such idle resources are typically inaccessible to other systems in a flexible manner \cite{Haykin2005CognitiveRadio}.

Thus, the core problem faced by traditional standalone systems is not merely ``insufficient total spectrum,'' but rather the lack of cross-system coordination in \emph{how} spectrum is used, leading to significant fragmentation in time, frequency, and space. In other words, while isolation between systems reduces the complexity of coexistence design, it also locks a large amount of potentially usable resources within closed service boundaries, making it difficult to achieve efficient reuse for global tasks \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC}. From a broader perspective, this creates a typical paradox: on the one hand, both communication systems and sensing systems complain about spectrum scarcity; on the other hand, a considerable amount of allocated spectrum remains discontinuously used, lightly loaded, or even idle in time and space. The essence of the problem is not the absolute absence of electromagnetic spectrum, but the inability of traditional management and system design paradigms to fully exploit its dynamic sharing potential.

This contradiction becomes even more severe in the era of high-frequency broadband systems. As communication systems expand toward millimeter-wave and even terahertz bands, more and more emerging services begin to exhibit stronger adjacency or even overlap with radar systems in the frequency domain \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated}. For example, around the 24~GHz, 28~GHz, 39~GHz, and 60--77~GHz bands, mobile communications, short-range high-speed access, automotive radar, imaging sensing, and industrial monitoring all demonstrate strong spectrum demand. In particular, in vehicular networking and autonomous driving scenarios, communication links and automotive radar are often deployed in neighboring frequency ranges, and they are highly coupled not only in spectrum occupation but also in spatial coverage, deployment location, and service object \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign,Kumari2018IEEE80211adRadar}. In this context, continuing to rely on rigid frequency isolation strategies will not only further compress available spectrum space, but also significantly limit the synergy potential across systems. In this sense, the spectrum occupation problem is no longer merely a regulatory issue of resource allocation; it is becoming a fundamental contradiction in the architecture design of future wireless systems.

Therefore, from the perspective of ISAC, reconsidering the spectrum problem does not simply mean competing for more independent frequency bands. Rather, it means abandoning the traditional idea of ``exclusive occupation'' and moving toward a new paradigm of ``on-demand sharing, joint design, and dynamic reuse'' \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC,Gonzalez2024integrated}. In precisely this sense, the spectrum occupation mode of traditional standalone systems constitutes an important practical background for the emergence of ISAC: only when communication and sensing are placed into a unified time-frequency-space resource framework and jointly designed can spectrum resources be transformed from ``statically partitioned scarce assets'' into ``dynamically schedulable foundations of integrated capability.'' This is exactly the logical starting point for understanding the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC.

\subsubsection{Sources of ISAC Advantages in Spectrum Sharing and Reuse}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-adv source of isac-spectrum}

The spectrum-efficiency improvement enabled by ISAC does not simply mean forcing communication and sensing to ``operate simultaneously in the same frequency band.'' Without unified design, mechanically placing the two services in the same band may instead lead to severe mutual interference and performance degradation. The spectral advantage of ISAC essentially arises from the joint organization and reuse of multi-dimensional resources such as time, frequency, space, and code. Under a unified system framework, the same spectrum resource can simultaneously serve information transmission and environmental sensing. Mechanistically, this advantage mainly comes from three levels: functional reuse of the same time-frequency resources, joint signal design for dual-function requirements, and network-level dynamic spectrum coordination.

\paragraph{1) Functional reuse of the same time-frequency resources.}
The most direct gain in spectrum efficiency comes from the dual-function reuse of the same time-frequency resource block. In traditional standalone systems, communication systems and radar systems usually occupy their own separate frequency bands and transmission slots. In ISAC systems, by contrast, the same transmitted signal can simultaneously carry communication data and serve as a sensing waveform \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Hassanien2015dual,Sturm2011WaveformDesign}. For example, in OFDM-based ISAC systems, the same OFDM symbol can convey modulated information to the communication receiver, while the echo reflected by targets can be used for range, Doppler, or angle estimation; in multi-antenna systems, the same transmit beam can provide directional communication gain to users and also illuminate the target region to enhance spatial sensing capability \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Gonzalez2024integrated}. From the perspective of spectrum usage, this means that resource blocks that would otherwise need to be separately allocated to communication and sensing tasks can now simultaneously perform both functions, thereby significantly reducing overall spectrum demand.

To illustrate this more intuitively, consider a simplified example. Suppose a communication system requires approximately 100~MHz of bandwidth to meet a target data rate, while a sensing system also requires approximately 100~MHz of effective bandwidth to achieve the desired range resolution. Under traditional independent deployment, the two systems would usually occupy separate spectrum resources, leading to a total demand of approximately 200~MHz. Under an idealized ISAC sharing mode, however, the same 100~MHz signal could support both communication and sensing tasks, thereby reducing total spectrum occupation. It should be emphasized that this example is only meant to illustrate the directional benefit of sharing; it does not imply that every practical system can losslessly achieve a ``halving of bandwidth demand.'' In real systems, communication and sensing differ in waveform preference, power allocation, peak-to-average power ratio constraints, reference signal requirements, and performance objectives, so some compromise is typically unavoidable \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC}. Even so, the spectrum saving and additional service capacity enabled by functional reuse remain among the most fundamental benefits of ISAC.

\paragraph{2) Deeper spectrum gains enabled by joint waveform and signal design.}
If functional reuse means that ``the same resource can serve two purposes,'' then joint signal design concerns ``how the same resource can perform both purposes more efficiently.'' The deeper spectrum advantage of ISAC stems from the joint optimization of design variables such as waveform, pilots, frame structure, beamforming, and power allocation. That is, communication and sensing requirements are considered simultaneously at the signal-design stage, rather than by simply superimposing two independently designed signals. Such joint design exploits the structural programmability of signals across multiple dimensions so that, within a given bandwidth, the signal can simultaneously provide both decodable information content and estimable sensing features.

OFDM is a representative example. Owing to its multi-carrier structure and strong engineering compatibility, OFDM is widely regarded as an important candidate waveform for ISAC. On the communication side, OFDM supports flexible subcarrier modulation, channel equalization, and multiuser access. On the sensing side, wideband OFDM signals can support range estimation through their frequency-domain structure and velocity sensing through phase evolution across symbols. If pilot patterns, subcarrier mapping, cyclic-prefix length, and frame structure are properly designed, then reference signals, synchronization sequences, or training fields that are already needed for communication can often also be exploited by the sensing module, thereby extracting target echo information without requiring fully additional spectrum occupation \cite{Ma2022WiFiSensingSurvey,Kumari2018IEEE80211adRadar}. In this sense, sensing functionality in some scenarios does not ``apply for an additional piece of spectrum,'' but is instead ``carried'' by the communication signal structure itself. Similarly, in MIMO-ISAC systems, spatial beams can support not only communication coverage and interference suppression, but also control the sensing illumination region and angular resolution through beam shaping, thus further improving the joint utilization efficiency of spectrum and spatial resources \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Liu2022ISAC}.

\paragraph{3) Dynamic spectrum coordination at the network level.}
Beyond resource reuse and joint signal design on a single link, the spectrum advantage of ISAC can be further extended to network-level resource scheduling. In future cellular networks, vehicular networks, industrial wireless networks, and low-altitude collaborative networks, both communication load and sensing demand are often highly time-varying, region-dependent, and task-dependent \cite{Saad2020Vision6G,Cui2024integrated,IMT2030Framework}. At some times, the network may face strong throughput pressure while sensing demand is weak; at other times, the network may need to prioritize environmental coverage, tracking accuracy, or abnormal-event detection. Under the ISAC framework, because communication and sensing share the underlying platform and resource management mechanism, the system can jointly schedule time slots, frequency bands, beams, transmit power, and computing resources according to service context, scene state, and operational objectives \cite{Zhang2020perceptive,Cui2024integrated}. For example, during periods of light communication load, more beam-dwelling time or denser reference signals can be allocated to support environmental sensing; when sensing demand is weak, more resources can be preferentially assigned to data transmission. Compared with traditional standalone systems, this kind of dynamic coordinated scheduling is no longer constrained by rigid cross-system boundaries, and can therefore transform fragmented and static available spectrum into a unified resource pool dynamically configured according to task objectives.

In summary, the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC does not arise from merely ``co-frequency operation'' at a single layer. Rather, it results from the combined action of multi-level coordination mechanisms: at the resource level, the same time-frequency resources are reused for dual functions; at the signal level, waveforms and structures are jointly designed to reduce redundancy and accommodate dual-task requirements; at the network level, the system dynamically schedules resources across tasks according to spatiotemporal service variations. These mechanisms together constitute the unique advantage of ISAC in spectrum sharing and reuse, and also explain why its spectrum-efficiency gain cannot be equivalently obtained by simply concatenating traditional standalone systems.

\subsubsection{Applicable Conditions and Boundaries of the Spectrum-Efficiency Advantage}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-spectrum-boundary}

While fully recognizing the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC, one must avoid an overly idealized interpretation---namely, the belief that as long as communication and sensing share spectrum, significant gains will necessarily follow. In fact, the spectrum-efficiency improvement of ISAC is not automatic. It is a conditional benefit that depends on specific scenarios, suitable architectures, and refined joint design. If the system requirements are strongly conflicting, or if the transceiver structure, interference suppression, and resource scheduling mechanisms are inadequate, then the theoretical advantage of spectrum sharing may fail to translate into practical performance gains. In some cases, the ``cost of sharing'' may even exceed the ``benefit of sharing.''

\paragraph{1) The inherent tension between communication-oriented and sensing-oriented signal requirements.}
First, communication and sensing do not have fully aligned preferences for signal structure, and this constitutes the primary boundary of the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC. Communication systems usually prefer signals that can carry as many independent information symbols as possible, in order to maximize spectral efficiency, user rate, and multiuser multiplexing capability. Sensing systems, by contrast---especially for range--Doppler estimation, target detection, and parameter tracking---often prefer waveforms with favorable auto-correlation, cross-correlation, and ambiguity-function properties, so as to achieve stable estimation and detection performance \cite{SkolnikRadarBook}. When the same signal is required to serve both communication and sensing tasks, the system inevitably faces a dual-objective tradeoff: stronger data loading typically means weaker structural controllability, whereas stronger sensing-friendly structure may constrain communication degrees of freedom and effective throughput. Therefore, spectrum sharing in ISAC cannot be achieved simply by ``placing two tasks in one band.'' Rather, the system must seek an acceptable balance among rate, bit error rate, detection probability, estimation accuracy, and other metrics.

This tension becomes especially prominent in some extreme scenarios. For example, when a communication task emphasizes very high spectral efficiency and high-order modulation under persistently saturated traffic, the degrees of freedom available for sensing reference structures and redundancy design are significantly compressed. Conversely, when a sensing task requires ultra-high range resolution, long coherent integration, and stable beam dwelling, the flexibility of the communication system in slot configuration, bandwidth allocation, and resource scheduling may be substantially limited. This indicates that the benefit of spectrum sharing depends strongly on whether communication and sensing demands are sufficiently compatible. If the two tasks differ too much in their objective functions, then the gains from joint spectrum use may be partially or even largely offset by the compromises required.

\paragraph{2) Self-interference and transceiver implementation constraints.}
Second, self\hspace{0pt}-\hspace{0pt}interference and transceiver implementation constraints are practical issues that are difficult to avoid, especially in monostatic ISAC. 
In many integrated monostatic architectures, the system must transmit communication/sensing signals and receive target echoes simultaneously in the same frequency band, which is similar to the strong self-interference problem in in-band full-duplex communications \cite{Sabharwal2014InBandFullDuplex}. Since the leakage signal from the transmitter is usually much stronger than the echoes from distant targets, insufficient analog isolation, digital cancellation, or compensation for hardware nonidealities will severely compress the dynamic range of the echo receiving chain. This may lead to degraded sensing SNR, difficulty in detecting weak targets, or even failure of range--velocity estimation \cite{Gonzalez2024integrated,Bliss2007MIMOFullDuplex}. In this sense, although spectrum sharing in ISAC theoretically improves resource reuse efficiency, it also imposes stricter requirements on RF front-end isolation, linearity, phase-noise suppression, and synchronization accuracy. If the implementation cost is too high or self-interference suppression is insufficient, the actual spectrum-efficiency gain will be much smaller than idealized analytical predictions.

\paragraph{3) Sensing performance requirements impose additional constraints on resource allocation.}
Third, the physical resolution requirements of sensing tasks themselves further constrain the upper bound of spectrum-sharing gains. Sensing performance is not characterized by a single metric, but is jointly affected by bandwidth, observation time, transmit power, beamwidth, and antenna aperture. In general, high range resolution requires larger effective signal bandwidth, high velocity resolution requires longer coherent observation time, and high angular resolution requires larger array aperture or finer beam scanning \cite{Richards2014,SkolnikRadarBook}. However, these requirements are not always aligned with communication-oriented resource allocation strategies. For example, to reduce latency, support fast scheduling, and serve more users, communication systems often prefer shorter frame lengths or more flexible slot structures; yet this may reduce the coherent integration opportunity for sensing and thereby limit velocity estimation accuracy. Similarly, to enhance multiuser fairness and throughput, communication systems may allocate spectrum in a more fragmented manner, which may weaken the satisfaction of sensing tasks that prefer contiguous wideband resources. Therefore, the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC does not exist independently of sensing resolution requirements. Its actual benefit must be evaluated under concrete task-level performance constraints.

\paragraph{4) Interference coupling becomes more complicated in multi-user and multi-target scenarios.}
Finally, in multiuser, multitarget, and even multibase-station cooperative scenarios, the benefit of spectrum sharing is further constrained by more complicated interference coupling. In single-user, single-target, or idealized link models, the gains of resource sharing between communication and sensing are often relatively intuitive. In practical networks, however, the system typically needs to provide differentiated services to multiple communication terminals while simultaneously detecting, resolving, and tracking multiple targets or scatterers \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Cui2024integrated}. In such cases, multiuser interference exists among communication users, clutter, sidelobe leakage, and parameter coupling arise among sensing targets, and communication and sensing tasks additionally influence each other through beam, power, and spectrum occupancy. As system scale increases, this coupling among ``multiuser--multitarget--multiresource'' interactions quickly invalidates simple spectrum-reuse strategies and requires more sophisticated mechanisms for joint beamforming, power control, pilot design, and task scheduling \cite{Liu2020JointRadarComm,Gonzalez2024integrated}. In other words, the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC in complex scenarios does not necessarily scale linearly with network size; on the contrary, it may encounter new performance bottlenecks due to the increasing difficulty of interference management.

In summary, the spectrum-efficiency advantage of ISAC should be understood as a system-level benefit with clear applicable conditions and boundaries, rather than as a universal conclusion that holds naturally in all scenarios. Only when communication and sensing requirements are sufficiently compatible, the transceiver architecture can effectively suppress self-interference, sensing resolution demands can be well coordinated with communication scheduling, and interference in multiuser and multitarget settings can be reasonably managed, can the benefits of spectrum sharing and reuse be fully realized. For this reason, later chapters, when discussing waveform design, transceiver architecture, and resource optimization for ISAC, must proceed from quantitative modeling and analyze performance boundaries under different system parameters and scenario conditions, rather than remaining at a purely conceptual level.

\subsection{Hardware Cost Advantage}
\label{subsec:ch1-isac-core value-hardware}
\subsubsection{The Problem of Hardware Duplication in Standalone Deployment}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-hardware-duplication}

When communication and sensing are carried out by two independent systems, it is usually necessary to build two relatively complete hardware chains in parallel, including antenna arrays, RF front-ends, analog-to-digital/digital-to-analog converters (ADC/DAC), baseband processing units, clock and local-oscillator modules, power supply and thermal management units, as well as installation platforms such as cabinets, towers, or vehicular mounting structures. In large-scale network deployment scenarios, such duplicated construction significantly increases both capital expenditure (CAPEX) and operating expenditure (OPEX) \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G,Liu2022ISAC,ITU2030}.

From the perspective of system composition, traditional communication equipment and traditional sensing equipment differ in their functional objectives, but they share a substantial number of similar hardware modules. For example, both cellular base stations and millimeter-wave radar systems rely on high-frequency RF front-ends, antenna arrays, beamforming networks, and high-speed digital signal processors. Under a standalone deployment scheme, these high-value components are often purchased, installed, powered, and maintained separately, while also occupying separate sites, spectrum, backhaul resources, and maintenance effort.

Consider the vehicular-to-everything (V2X) scenario as an example. If each roadside node deploys both a communication device (e.g., a C-V2X roadside unit) and a dedicated sensing device (e.g., millimeter-wave radar, camera, or LiDAR), then at least the following forms of duplicated investment will arise:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Duplicated front-end hardware:} communication and sensing devices each require their own antennas, RF chains, clock sources, and processors;
    \item \textbf{Duplicated infrastructure:} each system requires independent installation space, power interfaces, thermal design, and protective enclosures;
    \item \textbf{Duplicated network access:} each system requires its own data backhaul and network management interface;
    \item \textbf{Duplicated operation and maintenance:} each system requires separate monitoring, calibration, maintenance, and fault diagnosis processes.
\end{itemize}

In a medium-sized city, the number of nodes at intersections, roadside poles, parking areas, and tunnel entrances can easily reach several thousand or more. In such a case, the cost difference at a single node is amplified by network scale, making the question of whether hardware platforms are shared no longer merely a technical issue, but one that directly determines the economic sustainability of system deployment.

Beyond the initial construction cost, standalone deployment also significantly increases lifecycle cost. First, two sets of equipment typically imply higher total power consumption. Second, a larger number of devices increases the probability of faults and complicates spare-parts management. Third, sensing devices often require periodic calibration and environmental adaptation, which further raises maintenance costs. Therefore, in large-scale scenarios such as 6G, intelligent transportation, smart campuses, and the industrial Internet, reducing hardware duplication has become one of the practical drivers behind the development of ISAC.

\subsubsection{The Basic Logic of Hardware Sharing in ISAC}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-hardware-sharing}

The hardware cost advantage of ISAC fundamentally originates from the idea that \textbf{the same set of physical resources serves both communication and sensing simultaneously}. Such sharing may occur at multiple levels, including front-end hardware, platform infrastructure, signal-processing chains, and network control.

\paragraph{1) Front-end sharing}
At the front-end level, communication and sensing can share antenna arrays, beamforming networks, RF transceiver chains, and part of the clock/local-oscillator resources. This is particularly natural at millimeter-wave and terahertz frequencies, where both communication and sensing rely heavily on narrow-beam gain to overcome severe path loss. As a result, they have inherently common requirements for large-scale arrays, hybrid beamforming architectures, and wideband RF front-ends \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G, Wei2023Integrated}.  
For example, in a monostatic ISAC base station, the same phased array can generate communication beams toward users and sensing illumination beams toward the environment; the same power amplifiers, low-noise amplifiers, and mixer chains can also support dual-function operation under unified scheduling.

\paragraph{2) Baseband and algorithmic reuse}
At the baseband level, communication and sensing exhibit substantial similarity in algorithmic structure. Taking OFDM as an example, modules such as synchronization, FFT/IFFT, channel estimation, pilot processing, and beam training in communication systems are mathematically related to delay estimation, Doppler estimation, angle estimation, and environmental representation in sensing systems \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Sturm2011WaveformDesign}.  
Therefore, ISAC does not necessarily require a completely separate digital processing platform for sensing. In many cases, sensing capability can be added to the existing communication baseband processor through software upgrades, task scheduling, and limited hardware enhancement.

\paragraph{3) Platform-level integration}
At the platform level, existing cellular base stations, Wi-Fi access points, roadside units (RSUs), UAV platforms, and in-vehicle terminals can all serve as natural carriers for ISAC. Consider a cellular base station as an example. It already possesses several key features: multi-antenna or massive MIMO arrays; wideband RF and high-speed baseband processing capability; stable power supply, thermal conditions, and installation support; backhaul links to the edge cloud or core network; and built-in network management and clock synchronization mechanisms.  
These capabilities overlap strongly with the hardware foundations required for high-resolution environmental sensing. Therefore, compared with constructing dedicated sensing infrastructure from scratch, introducing sensing capability on top of existing communication platforms usually incurs a much lower marginal cost.

\paragraph{4) Network and data sharing}
The cost advantage of ISAC is not limited to the sharing of transmission and reception equipment; it also extends to the sharing of information links. Traditional standalone sensing systems often require dedicated data backhaul, target fusion, and control interfaces. In ISAC, by contrast, sensing results can be uploaded directly through the communication network to edge servers or control centers, while the communication links themselves can carry sensor data, target states, and collaborative control information. This reduces the need for additional networking infrastructure.

\subsubsection{Limitations of Hardware Sharing and the Associated Engineering Costs}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-hardware-limitations}

Although hardware sharing can significantly reduce overall investment, such sharing is not free of cost. The fundamental reason is that communication and sensing do not have perfectly aligned performance objectives, and in some hardware dimensions they even exhibit clear tension. Therefore, the cost advantage of ISAC should be understood as a \textbf{reduction in total system cost}, rather than as ``every individual component becoming cheaper.''

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Higher dynamic-range requirements:}  
In radar-like sensing, the target echo power is usually much lower than the transmit signal, and the echo strengths corresponding to different targets, distances, and radar cross sections may vary greatly. The receiver must maintain stable operation in the presence of strong leakage, strong short-range echoes, and weak long-range echoes, which often requires a higher dynamic range. In contrast, although communication receivers also need to handle received-power variations across users, they are not typically designed around extremely weak echo detection. If an ISAC platform must satisfy both requirements, then ADC resolution, automatic gain control, and front-end linearity may all need enhancement, leading to additional cost \cite{Wei2023Integrated}.

\item \textbf{More difficult transmit--receive isolation and self-interference suppression:}  
In monostatic ISAC, especially under simultaneous transmission and reception, transmit leakage, self-interference, and platform clutter may severely mask target echoes. To ensure reliable sensing, strong antenna isolation, analog-domain cancellation, and digital-domain interference suppression are often required. These measures increase the complexity of antenna layout, RF chains, and calibration circuits \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign, Hassanien2016signaling}.

\item \textbf{Stricter clock stability and phase-noise constraints:}  
High-accuracy range, Doppler, and micro-Doppler estimation are typically more sensitive to carrier stability, sampling-clock jitter, and phase noise. Although communication systems are also affected by phase noise, in many cases the effects can be mitigated through synchronization, pilots, and equalization. In sensing, however, fine velocity estimation, human-activity recognition, and high-resolution parameter estimation often rely more critically on stable local oscillators. Therefore, shared clock design in ISAC usually needs to satisfy the stricter precision requirement of the sensing side \cite{Sen2013YouAreWhat}.

\item \textbf{Tradeoff between linearity and efficiency in power amplifiers:}  
Communication systems, especially those using high-PAPR waveforms such as OFDM, require high linearity from power amplifiers. Traditional radar, on the other hand, often emphasizes high efficiency and high-energy transmission, and may prefer constant-modulus or low-PAPR waveforms. ISAC waveforms and hardware must jointly account for communication-friendliness and sensing-friendliness, making the coordinated optimization of power amplifiers, digital predistortion, and waveform design even more important \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign, Hassanien2016signaling}.

\item \textbf{Calibration and consistency-maintenance cost cannot be ignored:}  
Shared arrays and shared RF chains reduce the number of hardware modules, but they also make the system more dependent on precise calibration. Amplitude/phase errors across array elements, channel inconsistency, temperature drift, and mechanical misalignment affect not only communication beamforming but also sensing angle estimation and imaging quality. Therefore, large-scale ISAC deployment often requires more systematic online calibration and health-monitoring mechanisms, which translate into additional engineering complexity and software maintenance cost.

\item \textbf{Not all sensors can be fully replaced:}  
It should be pointed out that the hardware-sharing philosophy of ISAC mainly applies to \textbf{radio-based sensing}, such as range, velocity, angle, occupancy detection, and coarse-grained environmental awareness. For tasks that require very rich semantic detail, texture, color, or highly accurate 3D contour information, cameras and LiDAR still possess irreplaceable advantages. Therefore, in autonomous driving, robotics, or industrial inspection, ISAC is more likely to become a key component within a multisensor system, rather than completely replacing all other sensing hardware in every scenario \cite{Wei2023Integrated}.
\end{itemize}

In summary, hardware sharing in ISAC is not ``zero-cost sharing.'' Rather, it trades \textbf{a moderate increase in the design complexity of a unified platform} for \textbf{network-level, system-level, and lifecycle-level cost reduction}. From the perspective of engineering economics, as long as the CAPEX/OPEX savings brought by sharing exceed the additional costs of devices, calibration, and algorithms required to support dual-function operation, ISAC has a clear hardware-economic advantage. Later chapters will further discuss hardware nonidealities, transceiver architectures, and practical engineering implementation issues for real deployment.

\subsection{Deployment Efficiency Advantage}
\label{subsec:ch1-isac-core value-deployment}
\subsubsection{Why Future Networks Require High Deployment Efficiency}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-deployment-why}

The evolution target of future networks is no longer limited to ``providing high-speed connectivity.'' Instead, it is gradually moving toward the form of an integrated information infrastructure that deeply combines communication, sensing, computing, and control. Whether in the 6G vision, intelligent transportation systems, the low-altitude economy, the industrial Internet, or smart cities, these scenarios share several common characteristics: \textbf{more complex service environments, more diverse capability requirements, broader coverage, and faster service rollout}. Therefore, future networks must not only be ``more powerful,'' but also ``faster to deploy, more flexible to scale, and easier to upgrade.''

First, the application scenarios faced by future networks are expanding rapidly and changing dynamically. Take the low-altitude economy as an example. Services such as UAV logistics, inspection, mapping, security, and emergency rescue require not only reliable wireless connectivity, but also real-time sensing of targets, trajectories, and risks in the airspace. In vehicle--road coordination scenarios, roadside infrastructure must support not only information exchange between vehicles and roadside units, but also environmental sensing of road users, obstacles, and traffic states. In industrial Internet scenarios, mobile robots, automated production lines, and digital twin systems require the network to simultaneously provide highly reliable connectivity, low-latency coordination, and on-site status awareness \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G, Feng2020JointRadarCommunication, Shafi20175G}. These scenarios typically span wide areas, involve many sites, operate in complex environments, and exhibit rapidly changing service requirements. If the network construction cycle is too long, the implementation speed and scalability of new services will be severely constrained.

Second, the traditional model of ``independent deployment of communication systems and independent construction of sensing systems'' is ill-suited to the agile deployment requirements of future networks. Under such a model, the communication side must build base stations, access points, backhaul, and network management systems, while the sensing side must separately deploy radar, cameras, LiDAR, or other dedicated sensing facilities, and each side must independently complete site selection, power provisioning, installation, backhaul access, spectrum coordination, and operation and maintenance. Although this siloed architecture facilitates the separate optimization of individual functions, it creates clear problems in large-scale networked applications: duplicated site acquisition, power supply, backhaul, and installation conditions lead to high deployment cost and long construction cycles; heterogeneous interfaces and complicated cross-system coordination hinder unified management and rapid scaling; and separate planning and approval processes easily become engineering bottlenecks, further lengthening deployment schedules.

Third, future networks require stronger elastic deployment capability and on-demand upgrade capability. Many scenarios are not fixed for the long term, but instead exhibit clear temporary, phased, or emergency characteristics. Examples include network support for large-scale events, post-disaster emergency communications, temporary traffic control, short-term industrial production-line adjustment, and temporary opening of low-altitude flight corridors. These scenarios require the network to activate new capabilities and adapt services within a short period of time. If each newly introduced capability requires building an entirely new dedicated hardware system, then the network will struggle to adapt to such highly dynamic demands. Thus, high deployment efficiency is not merely a matter of cost; it is also directly related to the service agility, environmental adaptability, and commercial viability of future networks.

From the perspective of infrastructure evolution, high deployment efficiency is also closely related to network softwarization, virtualization, and platformization. Since 5G, an increasing number of network functions have evolved through software-defined mechanisms, cloud-native deployment, and open interfaces. This means that the expansion of network capabilities no longer depends entirely on large-scale hardware addition, but can instead rely more on functional augmentation and flexible orchestration over existing platforms \cite{Andrews2014, Letaief2019Roadmap}. Against this background, ISAC provides an approach that is highly consistent with the evolution logic of future networks: \textbf{using the existing communication network as the carrier and embedding sensing as an intrinsic network capability}. If sensing functionality can be rapidly enabled on existing communication infrastructure through software upgrades, parameter reconfiguration, and limited hardware enhancement, then the network can acquire new service capabilities without significantly increasing deployment complexity.

Therefore, future networks require high deployment efficiency because applications are expanding rapidly, service requirements change frequently, the traditional standalone deployment model is both costly and slow, and the ongoing trends toward capability convergence, software-defined operation, and platform-based architecture provide a practical foundation for more efficient deployment. In this context, the ISAC philosophy of ``communication infrastructure concurrently supporting sensing functionality'' is becoming an important technological path for improving deployment efficiency in future networks \cite{Liu2022ISAC, IMT2030Framework,ITU2030}.

\subsubsection{Mechanisms Through Which ISAC Improves Deployment Efficiency}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-deployment-mechanism}

The core mechanism through which ISAC improves deployment efficiency can be summarized as follows: \textbf{leveraging existing communication infrastructure, network capabilities, and operation-and-maintenance systems to rapidly add sensing functionality at low incremental cost}. This mechanism not only reduces duplicated hardware construction, but also shortens the entire chain of site planning, installation and commissioning, network access, and service activation.

\paragraph{1) Reuse of existing communication infrastructure}
Current cellular networks have already formed a mature infrastructure with broad coverage and dense site distribution. Many of the capabilities provided by this infrastructure overlap strongly with the basic requirements of wireless sensing. Therefore, if a base station can be enabled---through software upgrade, waveform reconfiguration, and limited hardware expansion---to provide environmental sensing while continuing to offer communication service, then the construction of a sensing network no longer needs to start from the full sequence of ``site application--equipment procurement--installation--integration and debugging.'' Instead, sensing can be quickly overlaid on the existing network.

From an engineering perspective, this approach brings at least four deployment benefits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reduced need for new sites:} sensing functionality can be attached to existing sites, avoiding the need to build large numbers of independent sensing locations;
    \item \textbf{Reduced civil-engineering and support cycles:} existing power supply, cooling, cabinets, towers, and backhaul resources can be directly reused;
    \item \textbf{Reduced system-integration workload:} sensing functions can be connected to existing network management, orchestration, and edge platforms;
    \item \textbf{Reduced approval and coordination complexity:} fewer new devices mean fewer additional site approvals, construction coordination steps, and operation-and-maintenance access procedures.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{2) Unified planning for newly deployed nodes}
For newly built networks or newly expanded areas, ISAC can also improve deployment efficiency through ``unified planning, unified construction, and unified activation.'' If communication and sensing are carried on the same platform, then site selection, coverage planning, power design, backhaul access, edge processing, and network management can all be completed within the same engineering workflow, rather than being separately planned and built for communication and sensing.  
The advantage of such unified planning lies not only in shortening the construction cycle, but also in reducing cross-system coordination and the difficulty of later retrofitting. This is especially beneficial in scenarios such as roads, campuses, ports, airports, and mines, where unified deployment is often more feasible than ``building communication first and retrofitting sensing later.''

\paragraph{3) Faster service rollout}
From the perspective of service activation, ISAC significantly lowers the threshold for introducing new sensing services. Under the traditional model, if a certain area requires new capabilities such as UAV supervision, traffic-state monitoring, or perimeter environmental awareness, it is usually necessary to re-plan dedicated sensing nodes, deploy backhaul links, and establish separate data processing and operation workflows. Under the ISAC model, however, operators can first activate relevant functions on existing communication nodes, and then bring the capability online through software updates, parameter configuration, and limited hardware enhancement. In this sense, ISAC makes the addition of network capability more like a ``platform upgrade'' than a ``network rebuild.'' This is particularly important for emerging services that require rapid pilot deployment, rapid replication, and rapid scaling.

\paragraph{4) Deployment efficiency enabled by software-defined evolution}
Another important deployment advantage of ISAC is its strong consistency with the software-defined evolution path of future networks. As wireless networks increasingly rely on software configuration, cloud-native baseband processing, open interfaces, and intelligent orchestration, many functions that previously required new dedicated devices are being transformed into functions achieved through software reconfiguration and resource scheduling \cite{Andrews2014,Letaief2019Roadmap}.  
Under this trend, if sensing can be provided as an intrinsic network function, then its activation process becomes more similar to the extension of communication functions, namely: on-demand activation, region-based enablement, policy configuration, and progressive upgrade. This makes ISAC not only superior to traditional standalone sensing solutions in deployment efficiency, but also more aligned with the future direction of network cloudification and hardware--software decoupling.

Of course, the above advantages usually depend on one important premise: the communication platform itself must possess a certain sensing potential, such as sufficient bandwidth, adequate array size, clock stability, and computing resources. \subsubsection{From Single-Node Efficiency to Network-Level Efficiency}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-deployment-network}

The deployment-efficiency advantage of ISAC is not limited to simplified construction at a single node. More importantly, it can generate significant scale effects and evolutionary benefits at the network level.

\paragraph{1) Efficiency gain at the single-node level}
At the single-station level, the most direct benefit of ISAC is the reduction of device count, installation procedures, and interface complexity. In the ideal case, one ISAC node can simultaneously perform functions that would otherwise be carried out separately by a communication base station and part of a sensing device, thereby reducing duplicated configuration of antenna systems, power interfaces, cabinet space, and backhaul access.  
This ``function integration and platform unification'' is particularly valuable in regions where tower loading capacity is limited, pole-mounted space is tight, or urban roadside equipment is densely deployed. It not only reduces the burden of individual site deployment, but also makes site appearance, energy consumption, and subsequent maintenance easier to manage.

\paragraph{2) Native support for multi-site collaboration}
At the network level, existing communication systems already possess relatively mature foundations for multistation coordination, including time synchronization, frequency synchronization, inter-site interfaces, resource coordination, handover management, and load balancing. These mechanisms were originally established to support continuous communication coverage and efficient scheduling, but they also provide important support for cooperative sensing among multiple sites \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G,Liu2022ISAC}.  
For example, multibase-station joint sensing usually requires high-precision time and frequency consistency among different sites, while cellular networks already widely employ mechanisms such as GNSS timing, synchronous Ethernet (SyncE), or IEEE 1588 Precision Time Protocol (PTP) to achieve inter-site synchronization. Compared with building an independent cooperative sensing network from scratch, ISAC can inherit these mechanisms directly, thereby significantly reducing the difficulty of network-level collaborative deployment.

\paragraph{3) Easier scaling from local trial to wide-area deployment}
ISAC also makes the network upgrade path smoother. Operators may first enable sensing on a small number of sites in hotspot areas, industrial parks, key intersections, or low-altitude pilot zones, verify the service value and technical feasibility, and then gradually expand to larger site clusters, eventually forming regional or even network-wide integrated sensing-and-communication coverage.  
This progressive evolution mode is easier to control than one-shot large-scale independent deployment of a sensing network for several reasons: investment can be phased, reducing early capital pressure; deployment can proceed while technical feasibility is being validated, reducing uncertainty in the technical route; expansion can follow actual service demand, improving resource allocation efficiency; and existing operation-and-maintenance systems can be reused, reducing organizational transformation difficulty.

\paragraph{4) From engineering efficiency to ecosystem efficiency}
More broadly, the deployment-efficiency advantage of ISAC is not only engineering-related, but also has ecosystem-level significance. If communication and sensing capabilities can be provided on a unified platform, then equipment vendors, operators, vertical-industry users, and platform developers can innovate collaboratively around unified interfaces, unified sites, unified data flows, and unified management systems \cite{IMT2030Framework,Letaief2019Roadmap}.  
This means that future network capability deployment may no longer follow the isolated expansion pattern of ``one function, one system,'' but may instead evolve into a platform-based expansion model in which new capabilities are continuously loaded onto a unified network foundation. Compared with traditional standalone-system construction, this model is more conducive to forming standardized, scalable, and replicable industrial deployment paths.

\paragraph{5) A necessary caveat}
It should be noted that the network-level deployment-efficiency advantage of ISAC does not mean that all sensing tasks can be seamlessly undertaken by existing communication networks. Network topology, site height, coverage direction, frequency-band characteristics, bandwidth conditions, and environmental blockage all affect sensing performance. In scenarios that require vertical field of view, extremely high-resolution imaging, or all-weather robustness, cameras, millimeter-wave radar, LiDAR, or other sensors may still need to be jointly deployed \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G, Wei2023Integrated}.  
Therefore, a more accurate statement is: \textbf{by inheriting the site foundations, coordination mechanisms, and operation platforms of communication networks, ISAC significantly improves the deployment and scalability of wireless sensing capability, but its ultimate benefit still depends on the specific scenario requirements and system design objectives.}

In summary, the value of ISAC in terms of deployment efficiency manifests as a multi-level advantage extending from individual nodes to full networks, from hardware to platforms, and from engineering to ecosystem development: it reduces duplicated construction at the node level, inherits existing collaboration mechanisms at the network level, supports progressive upgrading along the evolution path, and promotes platform-based expansion at the industrial level. These advantages make ISAC particularly suitable as a key technological direction for the rapid introduction of sensing capability into future networks.

\subsection{Advantage in Business Closed-Loop Capability}
\label{subsec:ch1-isac-core value-closed loop}

Unlike traditional networks, which mainly provide ``information delivery,'' Integrated Sensing and Communications (ISAC) can provide not only data transmission capability, but also direct and timely state information for upper-layer applications through real-time sensing of the external environment, target states, and service objects. In this way, ISAC supports a complete service chain from information acquisition and state understanding to task decision-making and execution feedback \cite{Liu2022ISAC, Zhang2021ISAC6G, ITU2030, IMT2030Framework}.  
In this sense, ISAC drives the evolution of the network from a ``connectivity tool'' to a ``platform for business closed-loop support,'' enabling the network not only to carry service data but also to participate more deeply in the service process itself.

The term \textbf{business closed loop} refers to the ability of a system to form a cyclic process around a service objective, consisting of ``state sensing--information transmission--analysis and judgment--control execution--result feedback--continuous optimization.'' In many future 6G-oriented scenarios such as intelligent transportation, the low-altitude economy, industrial automation, smart campuses, and public safety, high-speed and low-latency communication alone is no longer sufficient to support highly autonomous, real-time, and highly reliable operation. The network must also possess the capability to perceive the physical world and tightly collaborate with computing and control processes \cite{Letaief2019Roadmap, Saad2020Vision6G, ITU2030}.  
Business closed-loop capability is therefore important because many future network services will no longer follow the traditional mode of ``human initiation, human decision, human execution.'' Instead, they are increasingly evolving toward a new mode characterized by ``autonomous device operation, real-time system coordination, and network-involved control.'' Consequently, closed-loop capability is becoming a key indicator of the transition from a network that merely ``provides connectivity'' to one that ``supports intelligent service execution.''

\subsubsection{Limitations of Traditional Communication Systems}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-closed loop-communication}

The main objective of traditional communication systems is to achieve reliable information delivery among users, terminals, devices, and service platforms. Their design priorities are typically centered on metrics such as bandwidth, latency, reliability, coverage, connection density, and spectral efficiency \cite{Goldsmith2005,Andrews2014, Shafi20175G}. From a communication-engineering perspective, this framework is already highly mature and has achieved tremendous success in mobile Internet, Internet of Things, and broadband access scenarios. However, when viewed from the perspective of a \emph{business closed loop} rather than pure information transmission, traditional communication systems still exhibit a clear limitation: \textbf{they are proficient at delivering information, but they do not directly generate awareness of the external physical environment}.

More specifically, although traditional communication networks can carry data streams generated by cameras, radar, industrial sensors, vehicular terminals, and application platforms, the network itself usually does not directly acquire environmental states, nor can it actively infer key information such as target location, motion trajectory, behavior evolution, spatial occupancy, or event development. In other words, traditional communication systems are better at answering questions such as ``where should information be sent,'' ``how fast should it be transmitted,'' and ``with what reliability should it be delivered,'' but are usually unable to directly answer questions such as ``what is happening in the environment,'' ``where is the target,'' ``how is the state evolving,'' and ``how should the system respond next''---questions that are much more closely related to service understanding and service execution.

From the perspective of system architecture, environmental cognition and state judgment in traditional networks largely depend on dedicated sensing devices and independent processing platforms external to the network. For example, in vehicle--road coordination, environmental perception usually relies on standalone devices such as cameras, millimeter-wave radar, and LiDAR; in industrial automation, equipment-state detection and workstation recognition often depend on dedicated sensor networks and control buses; and in low-altitude scenarios, target detection, trajectory identification, and regional warning often rely on independent surveillance and detection facilities.  
In these systems, the communication network mainly uploads sensing data, distributes control commands, or maintains connectivity, while actual environmental understanding, state estimation, and decision-making take place in independent systems outside the network.

This separated architecture of ``sensing outside the network, communication in the middle, decision afterward'' was feasible in early scenarios with limited scale and relatively simple functions. However, when facing future scenarios characterized by high real-time requirements, high autonomy, and large-scale coordination, it reveals several structural problems.

\paragraph{1) Long processing chain and additional latency}
Under the traditional model, a complete service process usually goes through the chain of ``external sensing devices collect data--data are returned through the communication network to the edge or cloud--upper-layer platforms analyze and make decisions--control commands are generated--commands are sent back through the network for execution.'' Since sensing, transmission, computation, decision, and execution are distributed across multiple heterogeneous systems, the chain is long and introduces additional transmission delay, processing delay, and system interaction overhead \cite{Letaief2019Roadmap, Saad2020Vision6G}. In strongly real-time scenarios such as autonomous driving obstacle avoidance, collaborative robot control, and emergency-event handling, this long chain directly affects the response speed of the service closed loop.

\paragraph{2) Complex interfaces and weak cross-system coordination}
In traditional architectures, communication systems, sensing systems, and control systems are often built on different devices, protocols, and management platforms, leading to non-unified interface standards, heterogeneous data formats, and complicated synchronization mechanisms. This increases the difficulty of system integration and cross-domain coordination.  
Even when each individual subsystem performs well, the overall service closed loop may still be limited by interface bottlenecks, synchronization errors, and information loss.

\paragraph{3) Lack of global situational awareness at the network side}
Because traditional networks do not directly participate in environmental sensing, the network layer can typically observe only ``traffic states, connection states, and resource states,'' but cannot easily obtain ``target states, scene states, and task states.''  
As a result, network scheduling is usually optimized based only on communication-side metrics such as throughput, latency, and load balancing, rather than on task objectives themselves. For instance, the network may know that a certain roadside node experiences high traffic load, but not whether abnormal pedestrian crossing, vehicle emergency braking, or low-altitude target intrusion is happening nearby. Consequently, it cannot truly allocate resources or guarantee service from a task-oriented perspective.

\paragraph{4) Difficulty in supporting autonomous and closed-loop control}
Many future scenarios require the network to deeply participate in closed-loop control, rather than merely serving as a command-delivery channel. Examples include motion control in industrial Internet systems, cooperative collision avoidance in intelligent transportation, and trajectory conflict management in low-altitude networks \cite{Saad2020Vision6G, Liu2022ISAC}.  
If the network itself lacks sensing capability, then the service closed loop must rely on inputs from external systems, thereby reducing response speed, stability, and autonomy. In other words, traditional communication systems are naturally better suited to supporting ``open-loop information services'' than ``highly real-time autonomous closed-loop services.''

\paragraph{5) Limited capability for continuous optimization}
A business closed loop does not merely mean completing one round of sensing and one round of control; it also means that the system can continuously adjust resource allocation and decision strategies based on execution results. Since traditional communication networks lack direct observation of physical-world states, they usually find it difficult to independently accomplish continuous feedback optimization among ``sensing outcomes--service outcomes--network scheduling strategies'' \cite{IMT2030Framework, Letaief2019Roadmap}.  
As a result, in complex services, the network often remains a passive responder to upper-layer application requests, rather than an active participant in dynamic service optimization.

In summary, the fundamental limitation of traditional communication systems is not that their communication capability is insufficient, but that their system role is primarily defined as an \textbf{information pipe}, rather than as \textbf{part of environmental cognition and service closed loops}. For this very reason, the value of ISAC lies not only in improving spectral efficiency or reducing hardware duplication, but more importantly in making it possible, for the first time, to combine ``connectivity capability'' and ``environmental cognition capability'' within the same system, thereby laying the foundation for building truly closed-loop service networks.

\subsubsection{ISAC Forms a Closed Loop of ``Perception $-$ Transmission $-$ Decision $-$ Feedback''}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-closed loop-isac}

An important system-level value of ISAC is that it enables the network to form a service closed loop of ``perception--transmission--decision--execution--feedback,'' thereby evolving from a pure information-carrying platform into an intelligent infrastructure that can participate directly in service operation \cite{Liu2022ISAC, Zhang2021ISAC6G, Saad2020Vision6G, ITU2030}.  
Unlike the traditional model, in which sensing takes place outside the network, communication merely forwards data, and decision-making and control are distributed across independent platforms, ISAC shortens the chain among state acquisition, information sharing, task decision-making, and execution feedback by embedding wireless sensing into the communication network itself. In this way, it improves system real-time capability, autonomy, and continuous optimization ability.

From the perspective of system operation, the business closed loop supported by ISAC can generally be described by five stages: \textbf{perception, transmission, decision, execution, and feedback}. The core process is as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \textbf{Perception: acquiring environmental states and target information}

ISAC first exploits integrated signal design, dual-function waveforms, shared antenna arrays, and network-node collaboration to synchronously acquire environmental and target-state information during the communication process, such as target distance, velocity, angle, position, motion trend, occupancy status, and environmental changes \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign, Hassanien2016signaling, Liu2022ISAC}.  
Unlike the traditional model, which relies entirely on independent dedicated sensing devices, the key idea of ISAC is to \textbf{use the wireless signals, spectrum resources, and infrastructure already present in the communication system to endow the network itself with real-time awareness of the external physical world}. This means that the network no longer merely ``knows which users are communicating,'' but can further ``know which targets are present in the environment, what states they are in, and how those states are evolving,'' thereby providing first-hand state inputs for the service closed loop.

\item \textbf{Transmission: sharing state information and distributing control commands}

The state information obtained through sensing must be promptly shared within the network with relevant nodes, edge servers, control centers, and service platforms. At the same time, control commands generated by the decision system must be reliably delivered to the execution entities through communication links. Therefore, in a closed-loop service, communication no longer merely means ``sending user data to its destination.'' It simultaneously serves three functions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State synchronization:} rapidly delivering sensing results to network nodes or service platforms that need to react;
    \item \textbf{Coordination support:} enabling multiple nodes and terminals to share common environmental cognition results;
    \item \textbf{Command delivery:} transmitting scheduling and control information to execution entities with low latency and high reliability.
\end{itemize}
Thus, the communication link itself becomes the ``information-flow backbone'' of the closed-loop system, connecting environmental states, service logic, and control actions into a unified process \cite{ITU2030, IMT2030Framework}.

\item \textbf{Decision: scheduling, planning, and control based on sensed results}

After obtaining environmental state information, the network side or the application side can further perform resource scheduling, risk assessment, path planning, behavior judgment, task orchestration, and control optimization \cite{Letaief2019Roadmap, Saad2020Vision6G}.  
For example, in vehicle--road coordination, edge nodes can generate cooperative intersection-control strategies based on the sensed states of vehicles, pedestrians, and obstacles. In the low-altitude economy, airspace-management platforms can adjust trajectories and issue no-fly warnings according to target trajectories and conflict risks. In industrial automation, control systems can dynamically adjust robot task assignment and obstacle avoidance strategies according to equipment position and motion state. In public safety, platforms can generate alarms, coordination actions, or tracking schemes based on detected abnormal targets.

At this stage, sensing information no longer remains at the level of mere observation, but becomes a direct basis for resource control, task scheduling, and service optimization.  
Moreover, in ISAC networks, decision-making can occur not only at the application layer, but also at the network layer. For example, the network control plane can adaptively adjust beams, resource-block allocation, or sensing scan strategies according to target location and environmental state, thereby forming a \textbf{task-oriented network optimization mechanism} \cite{Liu2022ISAC, Zhang2021ISAC6G}.

\item \textbf{Execution: converting decisions into physical or network actions}

Once a decision has been generated, it must be translated into actual actions through the execution stage. The execution target may be either a physical entity or the network itself \cite{Saad2020Vision6G}.  
The former includes vehicle steering or deceleration, UAV trajectory adjustment, robotic-arm obstacle avoidance, access-control linkage, or alarm release; the latter includes beam reconfiguration, link switching, resource rescheduling, sensing-mode switching, and edge-task migration.  
The significance of identifying ``execution'' as an independent stage is that it shows that closed-loop capability does not stop at ``knowing what happened'' and ``deciding what to do,'' but must ultimately result in ``actually doing something.'' From the perspective of a service system, a closed loop is only truly established when the decision result can be stably and promptly applied to the physical world or the network system.

\item \textbf{Feedback: validating outcomes through continued sensing and refining strategies}

After a control action is executed, the target state and environmental conditions often change accordingly. ISAC can continuously sense the resulting changes and feed the updated states back to the network or application system, in order to evaluate control effectiveness, correct deviations, and optimize subsequent decisions \cite{Zhang2021ISAC6G}.  
This forms a dynamic cyclic process:
\[
\text{Perception} \rightarrow \text{Transmission} \rightarrow \text{Decision} \rightarrow \text{Execution} \rightarrow \text{Re-perception/Feedback}.
\]
Compared with an open-loop system, this closed-loop mechanism can more effectively suppress the impact of environmental uncertainty, target motion variation, and external disturbances, thereby improving the stability, robustness, and adaptability of service execution \cite{Saad2020Vision6G}.
\end{enumerate}

For pedagogical purposes, the closed-loop support capability enabled by ISAC can be summarized as shown in Table~\ref{tab:isac-closed-loop}.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Closed-Loop Business Support Process Enabled by ISAC}
\label{tab:isac-closed-loop}
\begin{tabular}{p{2.8cm}p{5.0cm}p{5.0cm}}
\hline
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Main Function} & \textbf{Typical Output} \\
\hline
Perception & Sense targets and environment states using integrated wireless signals & Distance, velocity, angle, occupancy, trajectory, scene status \\
Transmission & Share sensed states and deliver control information & State reports, coordination messages, control commands \\
Decision & Perform scheduling, planning, risk analysis and control optimization & Resource decisions, path plans, task strategies \\

\end{table}

Therefore, the closed-loop capability formed by ISAC is not a mechanical superposition of communication and sensing functions. Rather, through their deep integration, ISAC enables the network to connect the entire process from environmental cognition and state acquisition to information sharing, intelligent decision-making, task execution, and feedback correction \cite{Liu2022ISAC, Feng2020JointRadarCommunication}.  
This implies that future networks will no longer be merely ``channels for transmitting service data,'' but may become key infrastructures for sustaining complex service operation, dynamic optimization, and autonomous coordination. For high-real-time and highly autonomous scenarios such as intelligent transportation, low-altitude management, industrial control, and public safety, this closed-loop capability is precisely one of the core system-level values that distinguishes ISAC from traditional communication systems.

\subsubsection{The Significance of Business Closed-Loop Capability for Typical Applications}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-closed loop-applications}

The business closed-loop capability enabled by ISAC is of great significance for many typical future application scenarios. Its value lies not merely in the fact that ``the network gains an additional sensing function,'' but in the deeper fact that the network can become more tightly embedded in the service operation process itself, thereby continuously supporting the coordination of state sensing, information sharing, task decision-making, action execution, and feedback correction, and ultimately improving real-time responsiveness, autonomy, and collaboration efficiency \cite{Liu2022ISAC, Zhang2021ISAC6G, Saad2020Vision6G, ITU2030}.  
In intelligent transportation, the low-altitude economy, industrial automation, public safety, and similar scenarios, many critical services are not one-time information exchanges, but continuously evolving, dynamically adjusted, and repeatedly updated closed-loop processes. Therefore, closed-loop capability often plays a more decisive role in practical system performance than high-speed communication capability alone.

From a system-level perspective, the significance of business closed-loop capability is mainly reflected in three aspects.  
\textbf{First, it improves real-time response capability.} Since sensing, transmission, and control can be jointly completed within a unified network framework, the chain from state change to control response can be significantly shortened.  
\textbf{Second, it enhances autonomous operation capability.} When the network can directly acquire environmental states and participate in decision-making, many services no longer need to rely entirely on manual intervention or independent external systems.  
\textbf{Third, it reduces the cost of cross-system coordination.} Closed-loop capability allows communication, sensing, and control to be completed on a more unified platform, thereby reducing fragmented interfaces, information silos, and the overhead of multi-system coordination.  
This is particularly evident in the following representative applications.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Vehicle--road coordination: supporting the transition from information interconnection to a collaborative driving closed loop.}  
In intelligent transportation and vehicle--road coordination, road-environment perception is an important prerequisite for driving decisions and traffic control. Traditional vehicular networking mainly supports information exchange between vehicles (V2V) and between vehicles and infrastructure (V2I), but whether a vehicle can safely pass, whether it should slow down and yield, or whether there exist occluded targets or blind-spot risks still mainly depends on on-board cameras, LiDAR, millimeter-wave radar, or independently deployed roadside sensing devices. By integrating environmental sensing into the communication network, ISAC allows roadside units, base stations, or vehicular nodes not only to transmit state information, but also to participate directly in target detection, trajectory estimation, and road-situation updating, so that sensing results can be fed back more rapidly to vehicle control and traffic scheduling modules \cite{Kumari2018IEEE80211adRadar,Ma2026integrated}.  
The significance of this capability is that the network is no longer merely a ``message forwarder,'' but becomes a ``state provider'' and ``decision enabler'' within the collaborative driving chain. Through this closed loop, the system can more promptly acquire information about targets ahead, road obstacles, intersection conflict risks, and traffic-flow changes, and can then support driving assistance, route optimization, speed control, and intersection coordination, thereby improving both driving safety and road efficiency \cite{Ma2026integrated,Feng2020JointRadarCommunication}.

\item \textbf{Low-altitude economy: supporting an integrated closed loop of airspace sensing, scheduling, and control.}  
In the low-altitude economy, services such as UAV delivery, inspection, aerial surveying, security, and emergency rescue impose even stronger requirements on network closed-loop capability. Such services are typically characterized by large numbers of targets, rapidly changing flight paths, high airspace-conflict risk, and stringent timing requirements for control. As a result, the system must not only maintain communication links between aerial vehicles and ground platforms, but also continuously monitor vehicle position, velocity, heading, trajectory, and surrounding airspace dynamics \cite{Jiang2025integrated,Meng2023uav}.  
The business closed-loop capability provided by ISAC can organically connect \emph{target detection, state reporting, trajectory prediction, conflict avoidance, command delivery, and flight feedback}, allowing the network to play a more active role in low-altitude target recognition, trajectory supervision, airspace scheduling, and task coordination. Compared with the traditional mode in which sensing systems and communication systems operate separately, ISAC is more conducive to lowering system-coordination cost, improving airspace-management efficiency, and providing foundational support for the large-scale, standardized, and safe operation of low-altitude services \cite{Meng2023uav,Jiang2025integrated}.

\item \textbf{Industrial automation: supporting a closed loop of production monitoring, control execution, and feedback optimization.}  
One of the essential characteristics of industrial automation systems is their strong dependence on a rapid closed loop of ``monitoring--analysis--control--feedback.'' Modern factories require the network not only to connect devices and transmit data, but also to understand equipment operating conditions, production rhythms, changes in work areas, and environmental disturbances, and to trigger control feedback in a timely manner when abnormalities occur \cite{Akyildiz2020Industrial6G}.  
ISAC can use a unified wireless infrastructure to perform device localization, motion-state sensing, micro-vibration detection, intrusion recognition, and industrial data transmission at the same time, thereby providing more direct state support for production scheduling, anomaly alarming, collaborative robot control, and process optimization \cite{Kaushik2024integrated,Sturm2011WaveformDesign}. This means that the network is no longer merely a transport channel for industrial information flows, but gradually becomes a key bridge connecting the ``physical production process'' and the ``digital control system.'' Especially in applications such as flexible manufacturing, mobile-robot coordination, and digital-twin workshops, the closed-loop capability of ISAC helps improve system autonomy, reduce control-path latency, and enhance robustness and reconfigurability in complex industrial environments \cite{Kaushik2024integrated,Akyildiz2020Industrial6G}.

\item \textbf{Public safety: supporting a closed loop of situational awareness, risk warning, and coordinated response.}  
Public-safety services are typically characterized by suddenness, environmental complexity, multiple stakeholders, and time-sensitive response requirements. Whether in the protection of key areas, crowd monitoring, perimeter intrusion detection, disaster warning, search-and-rescue coordination, or emergency-event handling, the system must be able to detect anomalies in time, rapidly share information, and organize multi-party coordinated actions \cite{Ma2022WiFiSensingSurvey,Liu2022ISAC}.  
In such scenarios, ISAC can support target monitoring, behavior recognition, situation assessment, risk warning, and cross-department coordinated response more efficiently by integrating sensing and communication capability. The network not only reports incident information and delivers response commands, but also directly participates in environmental sensing and state updating, thereby forming a business closed loop of ``abnormality discovery--information aggregation--collaborative decision-making--action feedback.'' Compared with traditional separated architectures, this closed-loop mechanism is more conducive to improving real-time responsiveness, coverage flexibility, and overall response efficiency, especially in tasks such as large-event protection, key-area monitoring, and post-disaster emergency communication-sensing integration \cite{Ma2022WiFiSensingSurvey,Zhang2021ISAC6G}.

\item \textbf{Smart homes and smart spaces: supporting the transition from connected devices to services that understand users and environments.}  
In addition to the scenarios above, indoor environments such as smart homes, smart offices, and smart healthcare also highlight the importance of ISAC-enabled business closed-loop capability. Traditional wireless networks mainly provide device access and service-data transmission, while the acquisition of information such as human presence, motion trajectories, behavior patterns, fall events, and space occupancy status usually depends on additional sensors or cameras. In recent years, wireless sensing technologies based on Wi-Fi, millimeter-wave systems, and next-generation cellular networks have shown that communication signals themselves can be used for tasks such as human activity recognition, respiration and posture monitoring, and spatial-state understanding \cite{Ma2022WiFiSensingSurvey,Zhang2020device}.  
Once ISAC capability is introduced into smart spaces, the network can integrate user-behavior sensing, state uploading, service triggering, and outcome feedback into a continuous closed loop. For example, the system may automatically adjust lighting, security, air conditioning, and health-monitoring strategies according to indoor user location and activity state, and continuously refine service outputs based on subsequent sensing results. This shows that the value of ISAC lies not only in enhancing ``connectivity capability,'' but also in endowing the network with context-understanding capability toward people, devices, and environments, thereby supporting more proactive, personalized, and autonomous spatial services \cite{Zhang2020device}.
\end{itemize}

Overall, in the above representative applications, the fundamental significance of business closed-loop capability is that it allows the network to become more deeply embedded in the service process itself, thereby enabling the system to evolve from \textbf{seeing the environment} to \textbf{understanding the environment}, from \textbf{transmitting information} to \textbf{driving decisions}, and from \textbf{executing actions} to \textbf{continuous optimization}. From both pedagogical and methodological perspectives, this is exactly what distinguishes ISAC from the traditional simple concatenation of ``communication system + sensing system'': the goal of ISAC is not functional coexistence, but the construction of a task-oriented intelligent infrastructure with closed-loop capability through unified signal, resource, and system design.

\subsubsection{Business Closed-Loop Capability Is the Key for ISAC to Become an Intrinsic 6G Capability}
\label{subsubsec:ch1-isac-core value-closed loop-6g}

From the perspective of technological evolution and network-architecture upgrading, \textbf{business closed-loop capability} is the key step through which ISAC evolves from ``functional integration'' to an ``intrinsic 6G capability.'' For ISAC, only when sensing can continuously participate in service processes and play a stable role in network operation, resource organization, task execution, and feedback optimization does the fusion of communication and sensing acquire genuine architectural significance \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Zhang2021ISAC6G,Saad2020Vision6G}.

First, \textbf{business closed-loop capability transforms sensing from an auxiliary measurement function into an essential component of network service capability}. In traditional communication systems, the core task of the network is information transmission, while ranging, localization, target detection, and environmental observation are usually regarded as auxiliary functions serving specific vertical applications or external sensing systems \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign}. However, under the ISAC framework, sensing results are no longer merely ``environmental information passively provided,'' but become direct inputs to link establishment, beam management, wireless resource scheduling, mobility management, task orchestration, and application control \cite{Kaushik2024toward}.  
This means that the role of sensing has changed from conventional ``supplementary measurement'' to a foundational capability supporting network autonomy and service optimization. In other words, sensing is no longer an ``optional network add-on,'' but a necessary component for enabling the network to provide highly reliable, low-latency, environment-adaptive, and task-driven services. For 6G, this transition is emblematic: the network must not only transport bits, but also understand contexts, sense states, and support actions \cite{Saad2020Vision6G,Strinati20216G}.

Second, \textbf{business closed-loop capability is an important foundation for ISAC to evolve toward network-native intelligence and system autonomy}. 6G networks are widely expected to evolve from ``connectivity-oriented networks'' to ``intelligence-oriented networks,'' where the goal is not merely to improve peak data rate and connection density, but also to enhance the network's understanding of the physical environment, user behavior, and service tasks so that it can achieve higher degrees of self-adaptation, self-optimization, and self-management \cite{Saad2020Vision6G,Strinati20216G}. In this evolution, the core value of ISAC lies not only in spectrum sharing or hardware reuse, but more importantly in enabling the network to directly sense the external physical world and incorporate sensing outcomes into the network control loop.  
For example, the network may dynamically adjust beam directions and access strategies based on target location and motion state, optimize link configuration and handover decisions according to environmental changes, or trigger more targeted resource allocation and service assurance according to spatial situational-awareness results \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Zhang2021ISAC6G}. Without such a closed-loop mechanism, sensing information may remain only at the level of ``auxiliary observation'' and fail to become a true decision variable or control input for network autonomy. Therefore, business closed-loop capability effectively determines whether ISAC can rise from an ``integrable technical module'' to a ``network-intrinsic foundational capability.''

Third, \textbf{business closed-loop capability provides the logical starting point and architectural interface for ``ISAC+'' and for the integration of communication, sensing, computation, and control}. One of the important development directions of future 6G is to promote deep coordination among communication, sensing, computation, and control, so as to support complex task-oriented services such as intelligent manufacturing, autonomous driving, low-altitude coordination, digital twins, and human--machine interaction. If ISAC remains only at the signal-layer or physical-layer level, then its role is mainly reflected in waveform reuse, spectrum sharing, and hardware reuse---important, but still local optimizations \cite{Sturm2011WaveformDesign}.  
Once ISAC possesses full business closed-loop capability, however, its sensing results can naturally feed into higher-layer functional modules: sensing information can trigger edge-computing task offloading and model updating, computation results can further support task understanding and state prediction, control modules can generate execution strategies accordingly, and the results of control execution can in turn be verified and corrected through sensing mechanisms \cite{Shi2016EdgeComputing,Chen2024integrated}. In this sense, business closed-loop capability not only deepens the practical role of ISAC itself, but also provides the structural interface and methodological foundation for future evolution toward the integrated paradigm of communication, sensing, computation, and control.

Finally, \textbf{business closed-loop capability determines whether ISAC can truly become a foundational and core service capability of 6G networks}. The notions of ``native intelligence,'' ``native sensing,'' and ``intrinsic capability'' in 6G do not merely refer to adding several new functional modules to the network. Rather, they mean that these capabilities are embedded into the network operation mechanism itself and become underlying elements that support service execution and system optimization \cite{Saad2020Vision6G,Strinati20216G}. For ISAC, if sensing exists only as an isolated function, then even if it performs well in ranging accuracy, target detection, or localization, it still cannot deeply drive the evolution of the network architecture. Only when ISAC can connect sensing, transmission, decision, and feedback into a continuous cycle serving the full service process does it meet the conditions for evolving from a technical concept into a native network capability \cite{Liu2022ISAC,Kaushik2024toward}.  

Therefore, it can be argued that \textbf{business closed-loop capability not only embodies the essential advantage of ISAC over traditional communication systems and traditional separated sensing systems, but also determines whether ISAC can truly be incorporated into 6G network architecture as a foundational support capability for future task-oriented services}. This conclusion is highly instructive for understanding the key research directions of ISAC: future ISAC design should not only pursue the parallel improvement of communication performance and sensing performance, but also further investigate how sensing information enters the network decision chain, how it participates in service control, and how it continuously creates system-level value in closed-loop operation.
